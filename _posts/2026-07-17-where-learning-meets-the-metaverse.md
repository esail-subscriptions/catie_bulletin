How do you teach an ER nurse to palpate the abdomen of a pregnant patient, or learn where to position a stethoscope when listening to someone's lungs, if there's no actual patient available to practice on? At Texas A&M University, Dr. Jinsil Hwaryoung Seo has built an answer — literally.

An associate professor in the College of Performance, Visualization and Fine Arts and director of the Institute for Applied Creativity, Dr. Seo brings together an artistic background and rigorous technical skill to develop immersive virtual and augmented reality applications powered by artificial intelligence. Her institute draws specialists from multiple disciplines to push traditional education forward using high-fidelity 3D graphics, haptic feedback systems, and conversational AI. "I create virtual/augmented reality and artificial intelligence applications to support people in challenging situations in the real world," she explains. "Through the trainings in those environments, people can experience something not possible in the real world, but they can practice things in the virtual world. Hopefully, we can make an environment for training that can be repeatable and empower individuals."

Rather than asking students to memorize textbook passages or watch instructional videos, Dr. Seo's work places them inside the scenario entirely. Using VR headsets and controllers, students interact with motion-captured avatars — virtual patients, nurses, bailiffs — in realistic environments designed to mirror real clinical and professional settings. The result is what Dr. Seo describes as embodied learning. "Students learn specific concepts through this type of simulation-based experience," she says. "By physically interacting with objects in a virtual environment, their bodies engage in embodied learning, which enhances memory retention. As a result, they can more easily apply what they've learned to real-world situations."

**Building the virtual world, step by step**

Every training application begins the same way: extended collaboration between Dr. Seo's team and the faculty or subject matter experts who will use it. Before a single line of code is written, the team maps the content requirements, designs the environment, and plans how students will move through the module. Once rough visuals exist, SMEs review and iterate — sometimes weekly — until the prototype reflects what the classroom actually needs. Only then do visualization students step in to build the final 3D environment in Unity or Unreal Engine.

Dr. Seo's team spans graduate and undergraduate students with specializations in VR development, conversational AI, Unreal Engine, 3D animation, and motion capture. Together they bring virtual characters to life through a motion capture pipeline: capturing raw movement data, cleaning and processing it, then integrating it into applications so virtual patients and nurses move with natural, realistic fluidity. "We utilize a complex motion capture system," Dr. Seo says. "Then, 3D and animation artists collect motion data from a motion capture suit. After the shoot, the captured data is processed and cleaned up — then that motion data is integrated into the application, allowing our virtual characters to move in a highly natural and realistic way."

**From forensic nursing to anatomy labs**

Since 2020, Dr. Seo has collaborated with the Texas A&M College of Nursing to develop more than 12 VR programs, covering forensic nursing certification, sepsis emergency response, and parent-teen conversations about substance abuse. Forensic nursing students seeking specialized certification can now complete step-by-step patient exams inside a fully rendered examination room, guided by the app and monitored remotely by faculty — practicing trauma-informed care before ever working with a real patient. "The use of virtual reality has taken forensic nursing education to a new level," said Dr. Stacey Mitchell, clinical professor and director of the Texas A&M Health Center of Excellence in Forensic Nursing. "We have a new method for skill acquisition that is low stress. Our VR experiences reach our learners wherever they are, anywhere in Texas and beyond."

The collaboration has reached over 500 nursing students and providers across Texas, with presentations and peer-reviewed publications delivered at local, national, and international levels. VR also offers a practical advantage for under-resourced institutions: running a full gross anatomy lab is cost-prohibitive for many smaller schools, but a VR anatomy module — like the one Dr. Seo developed with Dr. Michelle Pine of the College of Veterinary Medicine and Biomedical Sciences — gives students the ability to identify, sort, and compare bones from two species, and even build a skeleton, from anywhere.

**Beyond medicine**

The applications extend beyond clinical settings. Working with the Challenge of Tarrant County and the University of North Texas College of Nursing, Dr. Seo's team built an AI-driven module that gives parents a space to practice difficult conversations about substance abuse with a realistic teen avatar — preparing them for real exchanges with their own children.

Most recently, Dr. Seo is developing VR reminiscence therapy for elderly patients with Alzheimer's and their families. Using old photographs of homes, gardens, and loved ones, the team recreates personal environments in virtual reality — transporting residents back to places they haven't seen in decades. "Maybe they've moved from where they were living 30 years ago, and now they're in the nursing home, often without familiar surroundings like their garden or personal spaces," Dr. Seo says. "So, utilizing old photos, we can recreate those meaningful environments in virtual reality — for them to be like, oh, I'm there, I'm in my house, like 30 years ago. These VR experiences not only immerse them in familiar settings but also stimulate conversation and help trigger vivid memories, enhancing emotional connection and cognitive recall."

Whether equipping a student nurse for her first forensic exam or giving a nursing home resident a few minutes back in her garden, Dr. Seo and her team are demonstrating something quietly profound: the virtual world, built with enough care, can make a very real difference.
